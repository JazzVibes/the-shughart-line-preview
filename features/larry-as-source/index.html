The Line That Points Back

A first feature for a wider railroad desk: S gauge, American Flyer, toy train, rail heritage, and private passenger rail communities, all held to the same source-first standard.

Review draft: this public preview shows the idea as a proof of concept. It is not Larry's approval or endorsement. The source route below is here so the facts, tone, and boundaries can be checked before any public launch.

There is a kind of railroad person who does not merely like trains as machinery, nostalgia, or decoration. He thinks in routes. He notices where the work begins, where the handoff belongs, where the bottleneck hides, where the story has lost its paperwork, and where a traveler needs a better sign.

That is why this idea starts with Larry Shughart.

Not because his name should be borrowed lightly. Not because a public website has any right to turn a living person's work, family, railroad, layout, music, or business into copy before he has reviewed it. Not because every public fact belongs on a public page.

It starts with Larry because a scattered train community needs a source-first way to keep its own map legible, and Larry's life sits at a rare crossing of those lines: professional railroading, S gauge, American Flyer, toy trains, rail heritage, Jacksonville railroad history, private passenger cars, music, civic friendship, and the long habit of making complicated systems understandable.

Originally from Pennsylvania, Larry attended the University of Pittsburgh and continued into graduate work at MIT. He spent 14 years at CSX, remained in the railroad industry as an economics consultant, led an operations research firm that developed railroad scheduling software, and later served as Global Lead for Transportation at WorleyParsons. His work has reached railroad projects in more than 25 countries, and he has testified as an expert witness in railroad-related cases.

That is not hobby-deep. That is dispatching-room deep.

But the professional resume is only one track. The public trail also runs through music stands, brass choirs, community concerts, and the kind of friendships that get built one rehearsal at a time. The First Coast Wind Symphony records Larry as a trombonist and part of Jacksonville's community music life. A good train schedule and a good brass section both depend on timing, listening, and respect for the line around you.

Then there is the railroad room. NASG's public page for Larry's Northern Florida Railway describes an active American Flyer layout: a 67-by-37-foot space, around-the-room with a peninsula, American S Gauge track, a whimsical tour of America, over 50 bridges, more than 500 buildings, and 83 turnouts. Those are not just statistics. They are evidence of a mind that has spent years making a world run at scale.

It is also a lesson for what this site could become.

The Shughart Line, if the name survives Larry's review, should not be a monument. It should be a depot desk. A bulletin board. A timetable. A source list. A dated archive. A clean place where you can arrive, look around, and find the original railroad room instead of getting lost in fragments.

The masthead is the promise: news, events, and stories connecting S gauge, American Flyer, toy train, rail heritage, and private passenger rail communities.

The local need is obvious. Florida railroad history, S gauge clubs, American Flyer collectors, toy-train rooms, regional shows, layout visits, music-and-train culture, Pullman heritage, private passenger car operations, and Jacksonville civic life do not live in one tidy file cabinet. They live on club pages, old PDFs, society notices, event pages, photo albums, manufacturer updates, museum calendars, orchestra pages, and conversations that disappear after the room clears.

The global need is just as real. Prototype meets, S-scale societies, toy-train show boards, Canadian show boards, UK S-scale notices, Australian RPM gatherings, European model-rail standards, NMRA regions, NASG pages, local clubs, rail heritage groups, and private passenger rail rooms already keep their own timetables. The problem is not that they lack owners. The problem is that the wider community needs a better junction.

That is the public purpose of The Shughart Line: not to replace those sources, but to point back to them.

In practice, the site would do old-fashioned work in a modern room. It would write short original notes. It would link to the original source. It would tell you where to confirm dates before traveling. It would keep a source directory. It would maintain a dated archive. It would label sponsor support plainly. It would create a correction and removal route. It would keep private notes out of public copy. It would let the person closest to the story correct the map before the site turns a lead into a claim.

The rule is simple: when the source knows better, the source wins.

That is why Larry matters here less as a subject than as a standard. A site built in his name, or inspired by his line, has to behave like good railroad work. It has to know the difference between a signal and a rumor. It has to know that a public link is not the same as permission. It has to know that some things can be admired without being exposed.

That is the Miracle on 34th Street principle translated into railroad language: send people to the right counter, even when the right counter is not yours.

Most people will never spend thousands of dollars on a premium private-car trip. Most people will never own a famous layout, publish in The Dispatch, build a railroad room, work on rail projects around the world, or sit as an expert witness in a complicated railroad case. But many people can care. They can follow a private passenger car's story. They can learn why a route matters. They can find a show. They can discover S gauge. They can understand why American Flyer and toy trains still have devoted communities. They can see that private varnish is not merely luxury, but preservation, operating discipline, hospitality, history, and craft.

A free public layer does not cheapen the rare experience. Done with restraint, it protects the magic by refusing to overexpose it. It lets more people stand near the story without pretending they have boarded the car.

For Jacksonville and Florida, this line could become a front desk for rail history, train shows, layouts, music connections, civic culture, Pullman heritage, and the people who keep those traditions alive. For S gauge, American Flyer, and toy-train collectors, it could keep source trails from vanishing into scattered pages. For private passenger rail and Pullman rooms, it could point interest back to the official source. For clubs, it could send attention back to the organizations doing the work. For families and newcomers, it could make the first step less intimidating. For sponsors, it could offer visible support without buying the editorial switch key. For the global railroad room, it could become a respectful junction: here is the meet, here is the society, here is the source, here is the original timetable, now go there.

There is no shortage of internet pages that trap attention. The better ambition is to earn trust by giving it away.

That is why the first station is permission.

Larry, mark the route. Tell us what is accurate. Tell us what is private. Tell us what is useful. Tell us what should never be public. Tell us whether this line should carry your name, lose your name, broaden into something less personal, or stop at the platform.

Because if The Shughart Line is worth building, it should be built the way a good railroad is run: with clear sources, known limits, careful handoffs, visible corrections, respect for the people who do the work, and a timetable honest enough that strangers can trust it.

A line is only useful if it takes people somewhere.

This one should take them back to the source.
